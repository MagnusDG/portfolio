## The brief

Master Data Management sits at the intersection of data governance, ERP integration, and enterprise architecture. Most introductory content either stays too abstract (useful for nobody) or goes deep into specific platform features (useful only for practitioners already sold on MDM).

The brief called for something that could orient a reader who understands databases but has never run an MDM program, while staying specific enough that a data architect could use it as a reference.

## What I wrote

The article is organized around a clear taxonomy: what MDM is, what it covers (the five data domains), where it applies, and how to implement it well.

The five-domain section is the technical core. Each domain — customer, product, vendor/supplier, location, and asset — gets its own treatment: what it contains, which systems depend on it, and what breaks when it's managed poorly. For product master data, that's SKU inconsistencies cascading into order processing failures. For vendor master data, it's duplicate payments and audit failures. These aren't hypotheticals; they're the actual failure modes that make MDM a funded program rather than a nice-to-have.

The application section covers data cleansing, governance, system integration, regulatory compliance, analytics, and DataOps. The goal was to show MDM as an operational discipline that runs across all of these, not just a data cleanup project.

The best-practices table closes the piece. Six practices, each with an explanation that names the failure it prevents rather than just describing the practice in the abstract.

## Process

Brief from the client's marketing team. Research was primarily primary (direct knowledge of ERP ecosystems and data governance frameworks from prior technical work). The article maps directly to how SAP, Salesforce, and cloud data platforms handle master data, which shaped both the examples and the terminology choices.
