## The brief

Alexander Thamm GmbH is a Munich-based data and AI consultancy. Their marketing team needed blog content that could hold up to scrutiny from a technical audience while remaining useful to executives who would never open a Gartner report.

The specific challenge with this piece: the concept of AI-ready data has real technical depth (data governance, lineage, annotation pipelines, adversarial attack surfaces) but the relevant audience skews toward CDOs and data strategy roles, not ML engineers. Getting that balance wrong in either direction — too shallow for practitioners, too dense for decision-makers — was the failure mode to avoid.

## What I wrote

The article opens with the core framing problem: most AI projects fail on data, not on the model. That line does two things — it signals to the technical reader that this won't be superficial, and it tells the executive where to pay attention.

The structure follows a deliberate arc:

1. Define AI-ready data precisely and distinguish it from "clean data" (a common conflation that causes real problems in AI projects)
2. Make the case that traditional data quality standards — which actively remove outliers — can produce training data that performs well in evaluation and fails in production
3. Walk through the four conditions (unified, governed, secure, supported) with enough specificity to be a working checklist
4. Close on the continuous operational nature of data readiness, not a one-time prep step

The reference table at the end synthesizes IBM and Gartner frameworks into a single working reference, with a column specifically noting where Gartner's use-case-specific framing diverges from general data quality standards.

## Process

Brief from the client's marketing team. Research phase covered IBM's AI-ready data documentation, two Gartner reports, and the IBM IBV 2024/2025 CEO study for specific statistics. Personal experience from ML research informed the section on what AI systems actually need from data versus what traditional BI pipelines deliver.

Draft went through Grammarly for mechanical editing. Final layout in Canva per the client's design system.
